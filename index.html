The bar lengths tell the story

Right now we pay $681/mo via direct debit — but our real usage is only $358/mo. The extra $323/mo is building a credit buffer at EnergyAustralia.

With either battery option, the monthly cost is slightly higher than what we'd pay just using electricity today — for 5 years. Then it drops dramatically, for the next 5+ years.

The deal isn't "magic instant savings." It's "five years of about the same, then much cheaper for a decade-plus."

The winter problem

Teresa's question — "how many more panels would we need for winter?" — is the right one to ask, because it exposes a structural limitation of solar in NSW.

To actually cover our winter consumption with solar, we'd need roughly three times our current array — about 60 panels total. That's not realistic (roof, inverter, network export caps all become limiting). Even if it were, in summer we'd be making 6× what we use and selling it back at 4¢ — a different kind of waste.

The right approach for winter

You can't out-solar a Hunter winter.

The real lever for winter bills isn't more generation — it's less consumption. Look at the August bill: 26 kWh/day of off-peak overnight usage. That's almost certainly the resistive electric hot water system heating up.

A heat pump hot water unit (~$3–4.5k after rebates) would cut that load by ~70% — saving more than the extra 11 panels would, at half the cost. Heat pump aircon for heating, plus draught seals, would do more again.

If we want winter bills lower, we electrify our heat loads with heat pumps. Then the existing solar + battery starts to actually cover winter.

iv.

Before we sign anything

Five concrete things to nail down. Each one is either free money on the table or a way to protect the deal we make.

Switch our EnergyAustralia plan this week

The October bill shows we could save up to $1,482/year on a different plan. Free money. Do this regardless of what we choose on the battery.

Get ONE more quote — Sungrow battery on our existing inverter

Ask for a 17 kWh Sungrow SBR or SBH installed on the existing Sungrow SG8K-D inverter, no new panels. If it comes in under ~$13k, take that — same-brand integration, 10+ years AU track record, no wasteful inverter swap.

Get the VPP terms in writing on one page

The $1,500 VPP subsidy has early-termination fees. We need: lock-in period (years), exit fee schedule, event cap per year, minimum reserve kept for us during a discharge event.

Get "17.97 kWh" written on the contract

Not the model number — the actual usable capacity, in writing. The line items only show one module, so we want this explicit.

Ask for the cash price

Brighte's 0% finance often has merchant fees baked in. Ask: "What's the discount if we pay cash?" Could be $1,000–1,500 in our pocket.

where I'd land

The battery — almost certainly yes.
The panels — only if we're electrifying.

The core deal — 17.97 kWh battery, ~$15k after rebates, six-year payback inside a ten-year warranty — is genuinely worth doing. We're throwing away ~$1,400/year right now in solar surplus at 4¢ feed-in. The battery exists to fix that exact problem, and it does, fast.

The panels are a different question. Long-term they pencil out positive (~$21k benefit over 25 years if feed-in tariffs hold). But 75% of the new generation would go to 4¢ exports, and NSW feed-in tariffs are heading downward — already dropped from 5¢ to 4¢ between our two bills. The math is OK, not great.

The panels become strongly worth it if we're going to electrify — EV in the next 2–3 years, heat pump hot water, heat pump heating. That doubles the panel value. Without that commitment, the marginal $6k is better spent elsewhere — likely on heat pump hot water, which would do more for our winter bills than the panels ever will.

My recommendation: Switch the plan this week. Get the Sungrow comparison quote. Then take whichever battery comes in cheaper, without the panels — unless we genuinely plan to electrify soon. Don't sign tonight.
